Expanding Focus & Alignment

A Modern Imperative for Project Management Professionals

Definition: Beyond Tactical Execution

Expanding Focus and Alignment is the strategic evolution of project management from a purely tactical discipline focused on outputs (scope, time, cost) to a holistic, value-driven practice. It emphasizes ensuring that every project directly contributes to the organization's overarching strategic objectives, creating measurable value, and adapting to the complex interplay of internal and external systems.

This shift is critical because projects that are executed perfectly but are misaligned with strategy can still fail to deliver meaningful business results. As noted by the Project Management Institute (PMI), a good project should reflect not only tactical excellence but also strategic importance. Expanding focus and alignment bridges the common gap between executive strategy and project execution.

Key Components

Mastering this skill requires integrating several core components into the project lifecycle, reflecting the latest guidance from standards like the PMBOK® Guide – Eighth Edition.

1. Strategic Alignment

The foundational practice of connecting project goals with organizational strategy. This involves using tools like the Balanced Scorecard and Project Portfolio Management (PPM) to select, prioritize, and fund projects that offer the highest strategic value. The goal is to move from a collection of desired projects to a focused portfolio that drives strategic goals forward.

2. Systems Thinking & Holistic View

A core principle of PMBOK® 8, this involves understanding that a project is part of a larger, interconnected system. Decisions must consider impacts on the wider organization, market, and environment. This mindset helps project managers anticipate unintended consequences and identify high-leverage points for effective change.

3. Value-Driven Focus

Success is measured by the outcomes and value delivered to stakeholders, not just by completing tasks. This requires a shift from focusing on outputs to ensuring the project creates benefits that justify the investment. PMBOK® 8 strongly emphasizes that value delivery and benefits realization are paramount.

4. Enhanced Stakeholder Engagement

Moving beyond simple communication to active collaboration. This includes analyzing stakeholder influence and interest, developing tailored engagement plans, and establishing clear feedback loops. The goal is to build consensus, manage expectations proactively, and ensure all key parties are aligned throughout the project lifecycle.

5. Adaptive Planning & Execution

Recognizing that no single methodology fits all projects. This component involves selecting and tailoring the right approach—be it predictive (Waterfall), adaptive (Agile), or hybrid. Frameworks like Scrum and Kanban provide the flexibility to respond to change while maintaining focus on the Sprint Goal or continuous flow, ensuring alignment is maintained even in dynamic environments.

6. Performance Measurement with KPIs

Using Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) that measure strategic contribution, not just operational efficiency. While traditional metrics (On-Time Completion, Budget vs. Actual) are important, a broader focus includes KPIs like Strategic Alignment Index, Stakeholder Satisfaction, and Benefits Realization to provide a true picture of project value.

Importance: The Strategic Advantage

Effective focus and alignment transform the project manager's role from a task coordinator to a strategic leader. The benefits are significant:

  • Ensures Project Success: Aligned projects are more likely to receive sustained executive support, adequate resources, and achieve their intended business outcomes.
  • Optimizes Resource Allocation: By focusing on high-value initiatives, organizations avoid wasting resources on projects that don't contribute to strategic goals, as highlighted in PPM principles.
  • Increases Team Motivation: When team members understand how their work connects to the bigger picture, it fosters a sense of purpose and boosts engagement and accountability.
  • Enhances Organizational Agility: A culture of alignment and systems thinking allows organizations to respond more effectively to market shifts, technological changes, and competitive threats.

Best Practices for Application

Developing and applying this skill involves a combination of mindset, process, and tools:

  • Master Goal-Setting Frameworks: Use frameworks like OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) for strategic alignment and SMART goals for tactical execution. OKRs define ambitious outcomes, while SMART goals create clear, actionable steps for individuals.
  • Utilize Visual Management Tools: Employ Kanban boards and project dashboards to create transparency. Tools like Jira and Confluence help visualize workflows and connect daily tasks to strategic goals.
  • Conduct Regular Alignment Reviews: Implement regular check-ins (e.g., quarterly business reviews, sprint reviews) to assess progress against strategic objectives and adapt plans as needed.
  • Adopt a "Pre-Mortem" Analysis: Before a project starts, gather the team to brainstorm all the ways it could fail. This proactive risk identification helps ensure alignment and preparedness from day one.
  • Leverage AI and Predictive Analytics: Modern tools use AI to forecast delays, identify resource conflicts, and provide data-driven recommendations, enabling proactive decision-making to maintain alignment.

Real-World Scenarios & Case Studies

The application of expanded focus and alignment is evident in numerous successful projects:

  • Digital Transformation at Starbucks: The rollout of mobile ordering required tight alignment between technology, marketing, and in-store operations. By focusing on the strategic goal of enhancing customer convenience, the project succeeded in creating a seamless user experience that drove significant revenue growth.
  • PMO Implementation and Evolution: A case study from PMI shows a new PMO that initially focused on project prioritization. However, after aligning with senior leadership's true priority—effective execution of strategic projects—the PMO shifted its focus, led key projects, and demonstrated immense value, which allowed it to grow and later re-introduce standardized processes.
  • Infrastructure Restoration in British Columbia: After catastrophic floods, the Ministry of Transportation and Infrastructure used a PMBOK® Guide-based approach to restore a critical highway. By aligning all efforts on the strategic priority of reconnecting the corridor, they reopened it in just 35 days despite immense challenges, demonstrating flexibility and collaboration.

Special Focus: Alignment in Conflict-Affected & High-Stress Regions

In fragile and conflict-affected situations (FCS) like Gaza, Syria, or Sudan, project management transcends traditional frameworks. Here, "Expanding Focus & Alignment" means aligning with the most fundamental human needs while navigating extreme uncertainty. The strategic goal shifts from profit or market share to survival, resilience, and humanitarian impact.

Project managers must employ adaptive management, an intentional approach to making decisions in response to new information and a rapidly changing context. As noted in research on aid in fragile settings, this requires a "project compass" rather than a rigid map. Key strategies include:

  • Conflict-Sensitive Planning: Projects must be designed to "do no harm" by understanding local power dynamics, resource competition, and social tensions. This involves continuous context and conflict analysis to ensure aid does not inadvertently exacerbate conflict.
  • Hyper-Flexible Execution: Methodologies must be highly adaptive. A project to rebuild infrastructure, as seen in post-conflict case studies, might need to pivot overnight due to security risks or supply chain collapse. The focus is on achieving the core objective (e.g., providing shelter) through whatever means are currently viable.
  • Building Social & Team Resilience: In environments where physical infrastructure is destroyed, social cohesion is the primary asset. Projects must empower local communities and build trust. For the project team, maintaining morale is a strategic objective, requiring leaders to foster psychological safety and a shared sense of purpose amidst trauma and stress.
  • Focus on Micro-Wins and Core Functions: With long-term goals uncertain, alignment focuses on immediate, life-sustaining outcomes. Celebrating small successes—like restoring a single water pump or delivering one shipment of medical supplies—is crucial for maintaining momentum and hope.

In these contexts, alignment is not with a corporate strategy document, but with the principles of humanity, resilience, and adaptability.
